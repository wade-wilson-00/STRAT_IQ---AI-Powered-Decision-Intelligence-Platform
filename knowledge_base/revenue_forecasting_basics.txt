DOCUMENT TYPE: Conceptual Reference — Revenue Forecasting Fundamentals for SaaS
PURPOSE: Explains the core concepts, inputs, methods, and best practices for forecasting recurring revenue in a SaaS business. Use this document to answer questions about how MRR forecasting works, what metrics it depends on, and how to build reliable projections.

---

WHAT IS REVENUE FORECASTING IN SAAS?
Revenue forecasting in SaaS is the process of estimating future Monthly Recurring Revenue (MRR) and cash performance over a defined time horizon. Forecasts are built using a combination of historical revenue trends and current operating signals such as churn rate, expansion rate, and growth trajectory.

Unlike one-time sales businesses, SaaS revenue is contractual and recurring — which means it can be modeled with more structure and regularity. However, forecasts still carry meaningful uncertainty, especially over longer horizons.

---

CORE METRIC DEFINITIONS

MRR (Monthly Recurring Revenue):
The normalized monthly revenue from active subscriptions. MRR excludes one-time fees, professional services, and non-recurring charges. It is the primary building block of all SaaS forecasts.

ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue):
ARR = MRR × 12. It is used to express the scale of a recurring revenue business in annual terms. ARR is useful for investor communication and annual planning, but MRR is the correct unit for monthly modeling.

Gross Churn:
The percentage of MRR lost each month from cancellations and downgrades, before accounting for any expansion revenue. Gross churn is always a loss.

Net Churn (or Net Revenue Retention — NRR):
NRR adjusts gross churn by adding back expansion revenue (upsells, cross-sells, seat growth) from existing customers.
- NRR above 100%: expansion revenue exceeds churn. The existing customer base is growing on its own.
- NRR below 100%: churn exceeds expansion. The business is shrinking within its existing customer base, regardless of new logo acquisition.

---

COMMON INPUTS TO A SAAS REVENUE FORECAST

The following variables drive most SaaS MRR projection models:

1. Current MRR — the starting point of the forecast.
2. Historical growth rate — the recent monthly rate of new MRR added from new customers.
3. Churn rate — the monthly percentage of MRR lost to cancellations and downgrades.
4. Expansion rate — the monthly percentage of additional MRR added from existing customers through upsells or seat growth.
5. Marketing spend trends — forward-looking investment signals that affect new customer acquisition volume.
6. Customer Acquisition Cost (CAC) — used to estimate conversion efficiency from spend to new MRR.

---

HOW SIMPLE MRR PROJECTION WORKS

A directional MRR forecast compounds the effects of growth and churn month by month:

For each month:
  New MRR = Previous MRR × growth_rate
  Lost MRR = Previous MRR × churn_rate
  Expansion MRR = Previous MRR × expansion_rate
  Projected MRR = Previous MRR + New MRR - Lost MRR + Expansion MRR

Even simple models like this produce useful outputs when the underlying assumptions are explicitly documented, regularly reviewed, and compared against actuals.

Important: more complex models (e.g., cohort-based forecasts, ML-driven predictions) are not necessarily more accurate than simple models if the inputs are poorly maintained or assumptions are not reviewed.

---

FORECASTING BEST PRACTICES

1. Use scenario bands, not a single number.
   Produce at least three scenarios per forecast cycle: conservative (downside), base, and aggressive (upside). Single-point forecasts create false precision. Scenario bands communicate the realistic range of outcomes and help teams plan contingencies.

2. Separate projection from confidence level.
   The projected MRR number and the confidence in that number are two different things. Always document both. A high-confidence conservative forecast is more operationally useful than an optimistic forecast with weak foundations.

3. Re-forecast on a fixed cadence.
   For most SaaS businesses, monthly re-forecasting is the minimum. Fast-growing or fast-churning businesses may need weekly updates.

4. Document all assumptions.
   Every forecast depends on assumptions: growth rate, churn rate, expansion rate, pipeline conversion. These assumptions should be written down, versioned, and reviewed monthly by comparing forecast vs. actuals. Untracked assumptions become invisible errors.

5. Track forecast error by segment.
   Disaggregate forecast error by customer segment (e.g., SMB, Mid-Market, Enterprise). Aggregate forecast accuracy can mask systematic errors in a specific segment that compound over time.

---

COMMON FORECASTING PITFALLS

- Treating one-time revenue as recurring: Professional services fees, implementation charges, and one-time bonuses inflate apparent MRR. These must be stripped before modeling.

- Ignoring contraction within existing accounts: Downgrades from existing customers reduce MRR even without cancellations. A model that only tracks new ARR and full cancellations will systematically overestimate revenue.

- Overfitting to seasonal spikes: A strong Q4 driven by annual contract renewals does not mean Q1 growth will continue at that rate. Separate seasonal patterns from underlying trend.

- Failing to update assumptions after market changes: If macroeconomic conditions shift, a key competitor enters the market, or a major product issue occurs, historical growth and churn rates are no longer valid predictors. Assumptions must be manually reviewed and updated.
