DOCUMENT TYPE: FAQ Reference — SaaS Analytics and Business Intelligence
PURPOSE: Provides clear, direct answers to common questions about SaaS metrics, churn prediction, revenue forecasting, and AI-assisted analytics. Each question is answered with enough reasoning context so that a language model can use this document to generate accurate, well-grounded responses.

---

Q: What is MRR?
A: MRR stands for Monthly Recurring Revenue. It is the normalized, predictable revenue a SaaS business expects to receive each month from its active subscriptions. MRR excludes one-time fees, setup charges, and professional services. It is the foundational metric for tracking business health, modeling growth, and producing revenue forecasts. Because it is recurring and contractual, MRR is more predictable than transactional revenue and can be compounded forward in projection models.

---

Q: What is the difference between ARR and MRR?
A: MRR is the monthly figure; ARR (Annual Recurring Revenue) is MRR expressed in annual terms. The standard formula is ARR = MRR × 12. ARR is used for annual planning, investor reporting, and benchmarking against industry standards. MRR is more appropriate for month-to-month operational tracking, churn analysis, and rolling forecasts. Neither is "more accurate" — they represent the same revenue at different time scales.

Q: What is churn rate?
A: Churn rate is the percentage of revenue (or customers) lost over a defined period, typically measured monthly. Revenue churn rate = MRR lost from cancellations and downgrades ÷ total MRR at the start of the period. Customer (logo) churn rate = number of customers who canceled ÷ total customers at the start of the period. Revenue churn is the more operationally important figure for financial modeling. Even a 2–3% monthly churn rate compounds to 22–31% annual revenue loss, which has significant long-term impact on growth trajectory.

---

Q: What is NRR and why does it matter?
A: NRR stands for Net Revenue Retention. It measures how much revenue a SaaS company retains and grows from its existing customer base over a period, after accounting for both churn and expansion. NRR is expressed as a percentage. An NRR above 100% means expansion revenue (upsells, cross-sells, seat additions) from existing customers more than offsets revenue lost to churn. This means the existing customer base is growing without any new logo acquisition. An NRR below 100% means the business is shrinking within its existing base. NRR is often considered the single most important metric for evaluating the long-term health and compounding potential of a SaaS business, because it measures whether customers are getting increasing value over time.

---

Q: Is high growth always healthy for a SaaS business?
A: No. High growth can mask fundamental problems in unit economics and product value. A business growing quickly by acquiring new customers while simultaneously losing existing ones at a high rate (high gross churn) is running what is sometimes called a "leaky bucket" — it must constantly refill the bucket with new revenue just to stay flat. This type of growth is expensive and unsustainable because it requires continuously increasing sales and marketing spend. Healthy growth combines strong new logo acquisition with high retention and NRR. The combination means the compounding base grows efficiently without needing to replace lost revenue.

Q: How should teams act on churn predictions?
A: Churn prediction scores are only useful when they are connected to specific, defined workflows. The recommended approach is: (1) Assign each account to a risk tier based on its churn probability score (e.g., Low: <40%, Medium: 40–65%, High: >65%). (2) Map each risk tier to a specific Customer Success playbook — for example, a High risk account triggers an executive-level outreach and success plan review within 48 hours. (3) Track the outcomes of interventions over time: measure whether the intervention reduced churn probability and improved retention rates for at-risk cohorts. (4) Use that outcome data to improve both the model and the playbooks. A churn prediction that does not trigger a human or automated action has no business value.

Q: How often should revenue forecasts be updated?
A: At a minimum, forecasts should be updated monthly, aligned with the close of each monthly period so that actuals can be compared to the previous forecast. Fast-moving businesses — those with high growth rates, volatile churn, or rapidly changing pipelines — should update forecasts weekly. The key discipline is not just updating the numbers but reviewing why the previous forecast was wrong and updating the underlying assumptions accordingly. Updating a forecast by mechanically applying the same assumptions to new data without examining assumption quality produces false confidence in forecast accuracy.

Q: What are the most reliable leading indicators of churn?
A: The following signals most consistently appear before a customer churns:
- Falling product engagement: fewer logins, reduced feature usage, or declining session frequency compared to the account's own historical baseline.
- Low or declining NPS score: customers who score as detractors (0–6) are significantly more likely to churn than promoters (9–10).
- Billing friction: payment failures, failed auto-renewals, or accounts requesting billing holds often indicate organizational instability or budget review.
- Increased support pressure: a sudden spike in ticket volume may indicate unresolved product problems or frustrated users escalating internally.
- Low seat utilization: when active users fall well below licensed seats, the account sponsor has difficulty justifying renewal cost internally.
None of these signals alone is definitive. The most reliable churn predictions combine multiple signals in a model that accounts for their relative weights.

Q: Can AI-generated insights be wrong?
A: Yes. AI and machine learning models produce probabilistic outputs based on patterns in historical data. They can be wrong for several reasons: the underlying data may be stale or mislabeled; the model may not have seen patterns similar to the current situation during training; customer behavior may have shifted in ways the model was not trained to recognize; or the model may reflect correlations that no longer hold after product, market, or organizational changes. AI outputs should be treated as decision support tools — they surface risk signals that humans might miss at scale, but they require business context, recent knowledge, and human judgment to act on responsibly. Every AI-generated insight should be validated against recent actuals and domain expertise before driving major decisions.
